## Brake Pad & Rotor Replacement

**Difficulty**: Moderate | **Time**: 1-2 hours per axle | **Tools**: Jack, jack stands, lug wrench, socket set, C-clamp or brake piston tool, brake cleaner, torque wrench

### Steps
1. Loosen lug nuts while tire is on the ground (1/4 turn)
2. Jack up vehicle, place on jack stands, remove wheel
3. Remove caliper slide pin bolts (usually 2 bolts, 12-15mm)
4. Hang caliper with wire or bungee -- NEVER let it hang by brake hose
5. Remove caliper bracket bolts (if replacing rotors)
6. Remove old rotor (may need to tap with rubber mallet; remove set screw if present)
7. Clean new rotor with brake cleaner to remove anti-rust coating
8. Install new rotor
9. Reinstall caliper bracket. Torque to spec
10. Compress caliper piston with C-clamp (open bleeder to prevent pushing dirty fluid back, or crack reservoir cap)
11. Install new pads (apply brake grease to backing plate contact points, NOT friction surface)
12. Reinstall caliper and slide pins. Lube slide pins with silicone brake grease
13. Torque caliper bolts and slide pin bolts to spec
14. Reinstall wheel, torque lug nuts in star pattern
15. **Before driving**: Pump brake pedal until firm. This seats the pads

### Bed-in Procedure
1. Make 6-10 moderate stops from 35mph (don't come to a complete stop)
2. Make 2-3 harder stops from 45mph
3. Drive 5 minutes to cool without heavy braking
4. Avoid hard braking for first 500 miles

## Spark Plug Replacement

**Difficulty**: Easy to Moderate | **Time**: 30 min (4-cyl) to 2+ hours (V6/V8 with restricted access) | **Tools**: Spark plug socket (5/8" or 13/16"), extension, ratchet, torque wrench, anti-seize (controversial), gap tool

### Steps
1. Let engine cool completely (30 min minimum). Hot aluminum heads strip easily
2. Remove engine cover if applicable
3. Disconnect coil pack connector (press tab and pull)
4. Remove coil pack bolt (usually 8mm or 10mm)
5. Pull coil pack straight up. Wiggle if stuck, don't pry
6. Use compressed air to blow debris out of spark plug well
7. Remove spark plug with spark plug socket (has rubber insert to grip plug)
8. Check gap on new plug (verify against spec on emissions label or manual)
9. Thread new plug BY HAND first to avoid cross-threading
10. Torque to spec (see torque-specs.md)
11. Reinstall coil pack, bolt, and connector
12. Repeat for remaining cylinders
13. Start engine, check for misfires

### Tips
- Replace one at a time to avoid mixing up wires (older vehicles)
- On V6/V8 engines, rear bank may require removing intake plenum or other components
- If a plug is seized, apply penetrating oil, let soak, try again. Applying heat to the head (not the plug) can help
- Ford 5.4L 3V: Known for plugs breaking during removal. Use specific extraction procedure

## Serpentine Belt Replacement

**Difficulty**: Easy | **Time**: 15-30 min | **Tools**: Belt routing diagram (photo before removal), correct size wrench or breaker bar for tensioner

### Steps
1. Locate belt routing diagram (usually on a sticker under the hood, or take a photo)
2. Place wrench/socket on belt tensioner pulley bolt
3. Rotate tensioner to release tension (direction varies -- follow arrow on tensioner)
4. Slip belt off one pulley while holding tensioner back
5. Slowly release tensioner
6. Route new belt per diagram. Leave tensioner pulley for last
7. Rotate tensioner again, slip belt onto tensioner pulley
8. Verify belt is seated in ALL pulley grooves
9. Start engine, verify belt tracks correctly and no squealing

### When to Replace
- Cracks on ribbed side
- Missing chunks
- Glazed/shiny surface
- Squealing that doesn't stop (after checking tension)
- 60,000-100,000 mile interval (or per manufacturer spec)

### Also Check
- **Tensioner**: Should move smoothly and hold tension. Replace if it wobbles, is noisy, or doesn't spring back
- **Idler pulleys**: Spin by hand. Should be smooth and quiet. Replace if rough or noisy

## Battery Replacement

**Difficulty**: Easy | **Time**: 15-30 min | **Tools**: 8mm, 10mm, or 13mm wrench/socket, wire brush, battery terminal grease

### Steps
1. Turn off all accessories and remove key
2. **Disconnect NEGATIVE (-) terminal first** (prevents shorts if wrench touches body)
3. Disconnect POSITIVE (+) terminal
4. Remove battery hold-down clamp/bracket
5. Lift out old battery (heavy -- 30-50 lbs)
6. Clean battery tray of corrosion
7. Place new battery (correct orientation -- terminals must match cable reach)
8. Install hold-down clamp
9. **Connect POSITIVE (+) terminal first**
10. Connect NEGATIVE (-) terminal
11. Apply battery terminal grease or anti-corrosion washers
12. Reset clock, radio presets, window auto-up/down

### Notes
- Some vehicles require coding the new battery (BMW, Mercedes, some VW) to reset charge management
- Memory saver (9V battery to OBD port) can preserve settings during swap
- Group size must match (BCI group number on old battery label)
- CCA (Cold Cranking Amps) should meet or exceed OEM spec

## Air Filter Replacement

**Difficulty**: Very Easy | **Time**: 5-10 min | **Tools**: None (most vehicles) or screwdriver

### Steps
1. Open airbox (usually clips or screws on top)
2. Note orientation of old filter
3. Remove old filter
4. Inspect airbox for debris, clean if needed
5. Install new filter (ensure proper seal, no gaps around edges)
6. Close airbox, secure all clips/screws

### Interval
- Every 15,000-30,000 miles (more often in dusty conditions)
- Inspect at every oil change

## Thermostat Replacement

**Difficulty**: Easy to Moderate | **Time**: 30-60 min | **Tools**: Socket set, drain pan, new thermostat, new gasket or O-ring, coolant

### Steps
1. Let engine cool completely
2. Place drain pan under radiator. Open petcock to drain coolant below thermostat level (partial drain is fine -- usually 1-2 gallons)
3. Locate thermostat housing (follow upper radiator hose to engine)
4. Remove housing bolts (usually 2-3 bolts, 10-12mm)
5. Remove old thermostat and gasket. Note orientation (jiggle valve or bleed hole goes UP)
6. Clean mating surfaces of old gasket material
7. Install new thermostat with new gasket/O-ring
8. Reinstall housing. Torque bolts to spec (usually 15-20 ft-lbs for aluminum)
9. Close radiator petcock
10. Refill coolant (use correct type per fluid-specs.md)
11. Bleed air from system (some have bleed valve, others require running with cap off and squeezing hoses)
12. Start engine, warm up, check for leaks, verify temp gauge reads normal

## Wheel Bearing Replacement

**Difficulty**: Moderate to Hard | **Time**: 1-3 hours | **Tools**: Jack, stands, socket set, large socket for axle nut, breaker bar, torque wrench, possibly press

### Hub Assembly Type (most modern vehicles)
1. Loosen axle nut and lug nuts (with wheel on ground)
2. Jack up, remove wheel
3. Remove brake caliper and bracket (hang with wire)
4. Remove rotor
5. Disconnect ABS sensor connector
6. Remove hub assembly bolts (usually 3-4 bolts from behind knuckle)
7. Remove axle nut
8. Pull hub assembly off. May need to tap with hammer if corroded
9. Clean mating surface on knuckle
10. Install new hub assembly. Apply anti-seize to mating surface
11. Torque hub bolts to spec
12. Install new axle nut. Torque to spec (see torque-specs.md)
13. Reconnect ABS sensor
14. Reinstall rotor, caliper bracket, caliper
15. Reinstall wheel, torque lugs

### Press-in Type (older or some import vehicles)
- Requires bearing press (hydraulic or arbor press)
- Usually best left to a shop unless you have the equipment
- Knuckle must be removed from vehicle, bearing pressed out and new one pressed in

## Oxygen Sensor Replacement

**Difficulty**: Easy to Moderate | **Time**: 15-45 min | **Tools**: O2 sensor socket (22mm with wire slot), penetrating oil, anti-seize

### Steps
1. Identify which sensor from DTC (Bank 1/2, Sensor 1/2)
   - Sensor 1 = before catalytic converter (upstream)
   - Sensor 2 = after catalytic converter (downstream)
2. Disconnect electrical connector
3. Apply penetrating oil to sensor threads. Let soak 15 min
4. Remove sensor with O2 sensor socket (wrench on hex flat works too)
5. Apply anti-seize to new sensor threads (do NOT get on tip)
6. Thread new sensor in by hand, then tighten to 30-40 ft-lbs
7. Connect electrical connector (ensure it clicks)
8. Clear DTC codes
9. Drive to verify code doesn't return (may take 1-2 drive cycles)

### Tips
- Easier to remove when exhaust is warm (not hot). Metal expands, breaks corrosion
- If seized, apply heat with propane torch to bung (not the sensor)
- Universal sensors require splicing wires -- match colors per instructions
- Direct-fit sensors are plug-and-play (recommended)

## Alternator Replacement

**Difficulty**: Moderate | **Time**: 1-2 hours | **Tools**: Socket set, breaker bar (for belt tensioner), multimeter

### Steps
1. Disconnect negative battery terminal
2. Remove serpentine belt (see serpentine belt section)
3. Disconnect electrical connections from alternator (usually 1 plug and 1 nut on B+ terminal)
4. Remove mounting bolts (usually 2-3 bolts, sometimes from below)
5. Remove alternator (may need to maneuver past other components)
6. Install new alternator
7. Torque mounting bolts to spec
8. Reconnect electrical connections
9. Reinstall serpentine belt
10. Reconnect battery
11. Start engine, verify charging (13.5-14.5V at battery)

## Starter Replacement

**Difficulty**: Moderate | **Time**: 1-3 hours | **Tools**: Socket set, jack/stands (usually accessed from below)

### Steps
1. Disconnect negative battery terminal
2. Raise vehicle if starter is accessed from below
3. Disconnect electrical connections (B+ wire nut, signal wire connector)
4. Remove mounting bolts (usually 2 bolts)
5. Remove starter (may need to work around exhaust or other components)
6. Compare old and new starter (bolt pattern, nose length, terminal location)
7. Install new starter, torque bolts to spec
8. Reconnect electrical connections
9. Reconnect battery
10. Test start

### Shims
- Some starters require shims between starter and block for proper gear mesh
- If new starter grinds or whines, shim adjustment may be needed

## Coolant Flush

**Difficulty**: Easy | **Time**: 30-60 min | **Tools**: Drain pan, flush kit or garden hose adapter (optional), new coolant, distilled water

### Steps
1. Engine cool. Remove radiator cap
2. Place drain pan under radiator petcock. Open petcock
3. Let drain completely
4. Optional: Remove lower radiator hose for faster drain
5. Close petcock. Fill with distilled water
6. Run engine with heater on MAX for 10 minutes
7. Let cool. Drain again
8. Repeat water flush until water runs clear (1-3 times)
9. Close petcock. Fill with correct coolant mix (see fluid-specs.md)
10. Bleed air from system
11. Run engine to operating temp. Check level. Top off as needed
12. Check level again after engine cools completely

## Transmission Fluid Drain & Fill

**Difficulty**: Easy to Moderate | **Time**: 30-60 min | **Tools**: Socket set, drain pan, new fluid (correct spec), new gasket or filter if applicable

### Steps (Drain & Fill -- NOT flush)
1. Warm up transmission (drive 10 min)
2. Place large drain pan under trans pan
3. Remove drain plug (if equipped) or remove pan bolts starting from one side to control flow
4. Let drain completely
5. Remove filter (if applicable -- some are internal and not serviceable)
6. Install new filter and gasket/O-ring
7. Clean pan with brake cleaner. Check for metal in pan (fine particles normal, chunks bad)
8. Install pan with new gasket. Torque in cross pattern to spec (usually 96-108 in-lbs)
9. Refill through dipstick tube with correct fluid (see fluid-specs.md)
10. Add approximately 4-5 quarts (actual amount from drain & fill is partial capacity)
11. Start engine, shift through all gears with foot on brake
12. Check level with engine running, in Park, at operating temp
13. Add fluid as needed to bring to correct level

**WARNING on flushes**: Some transmissions (especially high-mileage ones that have never been serviced) can be damaged by power flushes. Drain & fill is safer. Multiple drain & fills (drain, fill, drive 500 mi, repeat) achieves near-complete fluid exchange safely.

## Fuel Filter Replacement

**Difficulty**: Easy (external) to Moderate (in-tank) | **Time**: 15-45 min

### External Fuel Filter (older vehicles, some trucks)
1. Relieve fuel pressure (pull fuel pump fuse, start engine, let stall)
2. Place rags around filter to catch fuel
3. Disconnect fuel lines (quick-connect or flare nut fittings)
4. Remove filter bracket
5. Install new filter (note flow direction arrow)
6. Reconnect lines. Ensure clips are fully seated
7. Reinstall fuse. Turn key to ON (not start) 3 times to prime fuel system
8. Start engine, check for leaks

### In-Tank Fuel Filter (most modern vehicles)
- Part of the fuel pump module assembly
- Usually replaced as a unit when pump fails
- Access may be via trunk/rear seat access panel or by dropping the tank

## CV Axle Replacement

**Difficulty**: Moderate | **Time**: 1-2 hours | **Tools**: Socket set, large axle nut socket, breaker bar, pry bar, mallet, jack/stands

### Steps
1. Loosen axle nut (with wheel on ground, 30-36mm typically)
2. Jack up, remove wheel
3. Remove cotter pin and castle nut from lower ball joint or tie rod (if in the way)
4. Remove axle nut completely
5. Remove brake caliper and hang with wire
6. Separate lower ball joint from knuckle (ball joint press or pickle fork)
7. Push CV axle shaft through hub by tapping with mallet
8. Pry inner CV joint out of transmission (it's held by a snap ring)
9. Remove axle
10. Install new axle -- inner end snaps into transmission (you'll feel/hear the click)
11. Feed outer end through hub
12. Reassemble ball joint, caliper, axle nut
13. Torque axle nut to spec (see torque-specs.md). Install new cotter pin if applicable

## Tie Rod End Replacement

**Difficulty**: Easy to Moderate | **Time**: 30-60 min | **Tools**: Pickle fork or tie rod puller, wrenches, tape measure or paint marker

### Steps
1. Measure distance from inner tie rod to outer tie rod end (or mark the position on the threads) -- this preserves approximate alignment
2. Loosen jam nut on inner tie rod
3. Remove cotter pin from tie rod end castle nut
4. Remove castle nut
5. Use pickle fork or puller to separate tie rod from knuckle
6. Unscrew outer tie rod from inner tie rod (count turns)
7. Thread new tie rod on same number of turns
8. Install into knuckle, torque castle nut to spec
9. Install new cotter pin
10. Tighten jam nut
11. **GET AN ALIGNMENT** -- This is mandatory after tie rod replacement

## Cabin Air Filter

**Difficulty**: Very Easy | **Time**: 5-10 min | **Tools**: None usually

### Common Locations
- **Behind glove box**: Drop glove box down (squeeze sides or release stops)
- **Under dashboard**: Access panel on passenger side
- **Under hood**: Near base of windshield, behind cowl panel

### Steps
1. Access filter housing (glove box drop or panel removal)
2. Open filter cover
3. Note airflow direction arrow on old filter
4. Remove old filter
5. Install new filter with airflow arrow matching
6. Close cover, reassemble

### Interval
- Every 15,000-25,000 miles or annually
- More often in dusty/polluted areas

## MAF Sensor Cleaning

**Difficulty**: Very Easy | **Time**: 10-15 min | **Tools**: MAF cleaner spray (CRC or similar), Torx or security Torx driver

### Steps
1. Locate MAF sensor (in air intake between air filter box and throttle body)
2. Disconnect electrical connector
3. Remove MAF sensor (usually 2 screws/bolts)
4. Spray MAF cleaner on the hot wire/film element (the tiny wire or plate inside)
5. Let air dry completely (5 minutes)
6. Reinstall sensor
7. Reconnect connector
8. Clear any codes if present

**NEVER touch the sensing element. NEVER use anything other than MAF-specific cleaner (no carb cleaner, no brake cleaner, no compressed air directly on element).**

## Throttle Body Cleaning

**Difficulty**: Easy | **Time**: 15-30 min | **Tools**: Throttle body cleaner spray, clean rag, screwdriver or socket for intake hose clamp

### Steps
1. Remove air intake hose from throttle body
2. Spray throttle body cleaner on butterfly valve and bore
3. Use rag to wipe carbon deposits (open butterfly by hand)
4. Clean both sides of butterfly valve
5. Wipe bore walls clean
6. Reconnect intake hose
7. Start engine (may idle rough for 30 seconds as cleaner burns off)
8. Some vehicles require throttle body relearn (varies by make):
   - Turn key to ON for 10 seconds, OFF for 10 seconds, repeat 3 times, then start
   - Or use scan tool for idle relearn procedure

**Note**: On electronic throttle bodies, do NOT force the butterfly open aggressively. The motor can be damaged.
